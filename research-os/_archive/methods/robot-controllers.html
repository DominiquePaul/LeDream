Robot Controllers

Low-level control laws for manipulation — from position control to impedance tuning for contact-rich RL

🎮
What Is a Controller?

The controller is the low-level robot control law that converts desired motion — from a planner, policy, or RL agent — into actuator commands (motor torques or currents). The RL policy outputs a target; the controller figures out the physical forces needed to reach it.

The policy decides where to go. The controller decides how hard to push to get there.

Position Control

The simplest mode: the controller drives joints to reach exact target positions (or Cartesian poses). Very stiff inner loop — the robot resists any deviation. Great for pick-and-place in free space, but brittle on contact: any position error during insertion causes force spikes.

🧲
Impedance Control

An impedance controller makes the robot behave like a virtual mass–spring–damper relative to a target pose. It does not strictly enforce position — it enforces a mechanical relationship between motion and force.

\[ F = \underbrace{K (x_{\text{des}} - x)}_{\text{spring}} + \underbrace{D (\dot{x}_{\text{des}} - \dot{x})}_{\text{damper}} + \underbrace{M (\ddot{x}_{\text{des}} - \ddot{x})}_{\text{inertia}} \]
K — Stiffness
How strongly the spring pulls back to target. Units: N/m
D — Damping
Resists velocity differences. Prevents oscillation. Units: Ns/m
M — Inertia
Virtual mass shaping. Often set to zero in practice.

Stiffness Tradeoff

The stiffness gain K is the single most important tuning parameter for contact-rich tasks. Getting it wrong dominates all other sources of error — sometimes more than the learned policy itself.

Compliant (low K) Medium Very Stiff (high K)

🚫 Too Stiff

  • Contacts become brittle
  • Small pose errors → force spikes
  • Chatter, jamming, or damage
  • RL policies become unstable

🚫 Too Compliant

  • Robot yields too much
  • Insertion or alignment fails
  • Insufficient contact force
  • Sluggish, imprecise motion

Contact-rich tasks need the sweet spot — compliant enough to absorb errors, stiff enough to accomplish the task.

💡
Intuition: What Does Stiffness Feel Like?

Stiff Controller

Robot behaves like a rigid metal rod. Pushes through obstacles with full force. Any contact mismatch = damage.

Compliant Controller

Robot behaves like a springy wrist. Absorbs contact gently. Can adapt to misalignment. Needed for contact-rich tasks.

📐
SERL: Hierarchical Control & Force Bounding

SERL uses a two-layered control hierarchy: the RL policy π(a|s) sends setpoint targets at ~10 Hz, while a downstream impedance controller tracks them at 1 kHz. Each RL timestep blocks 100 inner-loop steps. This mismatch creates a critical safety problem.

SERL Impedance Objective

\[ F = \underbrace{k_p \cdot e}_{\text{spring}} + \underbrace{k_d \cdot \dot{e}}_{\text{damper}} + \underbrace{F_{ff}}_{\text{feed-fwd}} + \underbrace{F_{cor}}_{\text{Coriolis}} \]

where e = p − pref, p is the measured pose, pref is the target from the RL policy. Fff is feed-forward force, Fcor is Coriolis compensation. This is then converted to joint torques: τ = JTF + τnull.

The Force Bounding Problem

If pref is far from the current pose (e.g. RL sends a big jump), the error e becomes large, generating dangerous forces on contact. Reducing gains kp hurts accuracy. SERL's solution: clamp the error so |e| ≤ Δ.

\[ |F_{\max}| \;\leq\; k_p \cdot |\Delta| \;+\; 2\, k_d \cdot |\Delta| \cdot f \]

where f is the control frequency (1 kHz). By choosing Δ you get a hard upper bound on interaction force — without sacrificing gain accuracy.

Why Controller Choice Dominates RL Performance (SERL)

SERL learns from real interaction on physical robots. The same RL policy can perform very differently depending on the low-level controller. For contact-rich manipulation, controller choice and impedance tuning can dominate performance — sometimes more than the learned policy itself. For example, in PCB insertion, an overly stiff controller bends fragile pins, while an overly compliant one cannot position the part.

OEM Controller Type

Most robot arms ship with a default position or velocity controller. Swapping to torque or impedance control often requires firmware changes or a custom driver. SERL found that impedance control is critical for contact-rich tasks — the OEM position controller made the same policy fail consistently on insertion tasks that the impedance controller handled easily.

🧲

Impedance ≠ Position

Impedance control regulates the force-motion relationship, not the position. This enables safe contact.
